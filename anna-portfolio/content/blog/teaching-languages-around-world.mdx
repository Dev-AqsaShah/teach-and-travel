I never planned to become a teacher. I planned to be a translator, sitting quietly in a booth, converting one language into another. Language as a technical skill, a tool.

Then I started teaching, and everything changed.

## The Student Who Changed My Philosophy

My first student was a woman named Elena — a 67-year-old German who had grown up speaking Russian at home with her parents, both of whom had emigrated from Leningrad in the 1970s. Her parents had both died, and she had forgotten almost everything.

She didn't want Russian lessons. She wanted *them* back.

That first session, when she managed to read a sentence her mother had written in a letter she'd kept for 40 years — I understood that I wasn't teaching a language. I was helping someone find a door back to themselves.

## What Different Countries Taught Me

**The Americans** were the most direct. If something confused them, they said so immediately. This made lessons efficient and honest. I learned to be clearer, to never assume background knowledge.

**The Japanese students** would politely nod when confused, not wanting to interrupt. I learned to pause more, to ask explicitly, to read the small signs of uncertainty that politeness tries to hide.

**The Germans** wanted to understand the *system*. Show me the rule, and I will master it. This pushed me to articulate the underlying logic of Russian grammar I had only ever known intuitively as a native speaker.

**My students from the UAE** were often learning Russian to do business with Russian-speaking partners. Precision mattered enormously. I learned that language is trust — a business deal hinges on whether your counterpart believes you understand them.

## Language as Empathy Practice

The most consistent thing I've observed across 8 years and 200+ students: people who learn foreign languages become more empathetic. Not just linguistically, but humanly.

When you struggle to say something in another language, you learn patience — with yourself, and then inevitably with others who struggle to communicate in yours. You stop taking fluency for granted. You hear the accent and wonder about the journey.

> "Every accent is a map of where someone has been." — Unknown

## Why I Travel

People sometimes ask why a language teacher needs to travel. The answer is that language only makes full sense in context. I don't just teach Russian words — I teach the Moscow metro, the *banya*, the way a Russian *babushka* gives advice that sounds like a scold but is actually love.

And I bring back what I learn. From Japan, I brought a lesson about *ma* — the beauty of silence and space — and it changed how I think about pausing in conversation. From Morocco, I brought a lesson about hospitality as a communication act in itself.

Travel has made me a better teacher because it has reminded me, over and over, that every person I meet knows things I don't. Including my students.
